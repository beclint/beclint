The Hausa are one of the largest ethnic groups in Africa, with a rich tradition of storytelling (tatsuniya). Their folktales often feature animals, spirits, and moral lessons that reflect the values of the community — honor, hospitality, and keeping one's word.

## Story Authenticity

"The Girl Who Married a Snake" is a well-known Hausa folktale, sometimes called "Binta and the Serpent" or "The Snake Husband." It follows the classic "animal bridegroom" motif found in many cultures but with distinctly Hausa elements: the baobab tree (a sacred symbol in West Africa), the harmattan wind, and the Hausa proverb about promises. The transformation of a snake into a prince is a common motif in Hausa folklore, reflecting the belief that spirits and animals can cross boundaries.

## What the Story Teaches

- **The sacredness of promises** — A word given is a bond that must be honored
- **Fear blinds us** — The chief's fear prevented him from seeing the truth
- **Trust over appearances** — The serpent was not what he seemed
- **Love requires faith** — Binta's love was tested by the whole world's disbelief

## Trigger Warnings / Content Notes

- **Mild:** Brief mention of fire/death (the prince is lost in flames — not graphically described)
- **Emotional:** Story of tragic love, loss, and broken trust
- **Suitable for:** Ages 10+ (with parental guidance for younger children)
- **Animal content:** A snake is a central character, but is portrayed positively
