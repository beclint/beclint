# The Girl Who Married a Snake

**Origin:** Hausa people, Nigeria (West Africa)
**Duration:** 4 minutes
**Tone:** Mysterious, tender, tragic

---

## Narration Script

### [0:00 - 0:30] — HOOK
*(Narrator: low, slow, reverent tone. A single deep drumbeat echoes.)*

**NARRATOR:**
In the heart of Hausaland, among the ancient city-states of northern Nigeria, there is a story they still tell by firelight...

A story about a girl who kept her word... even when her word was given to a serpent.

*(Pause. Sound of wind across dry savanna.)*

They say that in a village near the ancient walls of Kano, there lived a chief's daughter whose beauty was spoken of in seven kingdoms. Her name was **Binta**, which means "daughter of the one who is with me."

And Binta had a secret.

### [0:30 - 1:30] — SETUP
*(Narrator: warmer, storytelling tone. Gentle rhythm.)*

**NARRATOR:**
Every evening, when the harmattan wind carried the dust across the plains, Binta would walk to the great baobab tree beyond the village walls. She would sit at its roots and sing — songs her mother had taught her, songs of the old spirits.

But she was not alone.

From the hollow of the baobab, a great serpent would emerge. His scales shimmered like the waters of the River Niger under moonlight. His eyes held the wisdom of a thousand seasons.

And he spoke.

*(Beat. Narrator's voice drops slightly.)*

**NARRATOR:**
"Binta," the serpent said, "I have listened to your songs for seven moons. Your voice is the sweetest thing I have known in my long life."

Binta was not afraid. For the serpent spoke with a voice like an old king — gentle, ancient, and full of sorrow.

### [1:30 - 2:30] — THE PROMISE
*(Narrator: building intensity, leaning in.)*

**NARRATOR:**
One evening, as the sky burned orange and the baobab cast its long shadow, the serpent spoke again.

"I have loved you from the first note you sang," he whispered. "Will you be my wife?"

Binta looked into those ancient eyes. She knew the laws of her people. She knew what her father would say. But she also knew — deep in her bones — that this serpent was no ordinary creature.

"I will marry you," she said, "on one condition. That you never harm my people, and that you come for me when the moon is full."

The serpent coiled once, his scales catching the dying light.

"I will come," he said.

*(Pause. Narrator: quietly ominous.)*

**NARRATOR:**
And he did.

### [2:30 - 3:30] — THE WEDDING & TRANSFORMATION
*(Narrator: dramatic, slightly faster pacing. Sound of celebration — drums, ululation — then silence.)*

**NARRATOR:**
On the night of the full moon, the serpent came to the village. The people screamed. Warriors drew their swords. But Binta stood before them all.

"Father," she said, her voice steady as stone, "I gave my word. I will not break it."

*(Beat.)*

**NARRATOR:**
The chief's face twisted with shame and fury. But a Hausa saying is older than the walls of Kano: *"Magana ta fi ƙarfi" — "A promise is stronger than strength."*

He let her go.

Binta walked with the serpent into the wilderness. The villagers followed at a distance, torches flickering. And when they reached the baobab, something impossible happened.

The serpent began to transform.

His scales fell away like leaves in the wind. His body stretched and shifted. And where the serpent had been, there now stood a man — tall, radiant, wearing the crown of a prince from a kingdom no living soul had ever seen.

*(Narrator: wonder, awe.)*

**NARRATOR:**
"I was cursed by a jealous sorcerer," the prince said. "The spell said only a woman who would keep her word to a serpent could free me. For a hundred years, I waited. And you, Binta, have waited for me."

### [3:30 - 4:00] — THE TRAGEDY & MORAL
*(Narrator: slow, solemn, teaching tone.)*

**NARRATOR:**
But the chief's heart was hard. He could not believe what his eyes had seen. Convinced the serpent was a demon, he ordered his warriors to burn the baobab to the ground.

And in the fire, the prince was lost.

*(Beat.)*

**NARRATOR:**
The Hausa say: *"Wanda ya ci amana, ba shi da gari"* — "The one who breaks trust has no home."

Binta never married. She spent her days singing at the baobab's remaining stump, her voice carrying across the plains, waiting for a prince who would never return.

The story of Binta and the serpent is a warning and a gift. It reminds us that a promise made is a debt owed. That the truth is not always what it seems. And that love — true love — requires faith, even when the world calls you a fool.

*(Pause. Voice softens.)*

**NARRATOR:**
So the next time you hear the wind whisper through the leaves of an old baobab, listen closely. It might be a girl who kept her word... and a prince who was freed too late.

## Narration Notes
- **Opening:** Speak slowly, each word weighted. Let the silence breathe.
- **The Promise scene:** Binta's dialogue should be firm but gentle. The serpent's voice should be deep, ancient, slightly echoing.
- **Transformation scene:** Pace picks up. Wonder and awe.
- **Moral:** Slow down. Let the Hausa proverb land. Pause after it.
- **Closing:** Whisper-like, as if telling a secret.
