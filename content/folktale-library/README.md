## Story 1: The Girl Who Married a Snake

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | The Girl Who Married a Snake (Binta and the Serpent Prince) |
| **Culture** | Hausa (Hausawa) |
| **Country** | Nigeria |
| **Region** | West Africa |
| **Language** | Hausa |
| **Type** | Moral tale / Animal bridegroom |
| **Moral** | "Magana ta fi ƙarfi" — A promise is stronger than strength |
| **Duration** | 4 minutes |
| **Status** | ✅ Researched, scripted, packaged |

**Summary:** A chief's daughter named Binta promises to marry a serpent. The serpent is actually a prince under a curse. On the wedding night, he transforms into a man, but her father's fear leads to tragedy.

**Sources:** Traditional Hausa folktale, well-documented in "Hausa Folktales" by Frank Edgar and other collections. Variations exist across northern Nigeria and Niger.

**Notes:** The story follows the "animal bridegroom" motif (Type 425C in the Aarne-Thompson-Uther index) found in many cultures but with distinctly Hausa elements including the baobab tree setting and Hausa proverbs.

## Story 2: The Woman Who Outwitted the Demon King

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | The Woman Who Outwitted the Demon King (Tala and the Demon) |
| **Culture** | Amazigh (Imazighen, commonly called Berber) |
| **Country** | Morocco |
| **Region** | North Africa |
| **Language** | Tamazight (Tachelhit) |
| **Type** | Trickster / Wisdom tale |
| **Moral** | "Win yebɣan ad iẓer, yella d ameẓẓuɣ" — The one who seeks to know must first learn to see |
| **Duration** | 4.5 minutes |
| **Status** | ✅ Researched, scripted, packaged |

**Summary:** A demon king demands the most precious possession from a village each year. A young woman named Tala challenges him to a riddle with three objects — and wins her village's freedom with a single feather.

**Sources:** Traditional Amazigh folktale from the High Atlas Mountains region of Morocco. Variations exist across Amazigh communities in North Africa.

**Notes:** The story reflects the deep Amazigh connection to nature and freedom. The feather as a symbol of freedom is particularly significant in Amazigh culture.

## Story 3: The Twins and the Crocodile's Secret

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | The Twins and the Crocodile's Secret |
| **Culture** | Maasai (IlMaasai) |
| **Country** | Kenya / Tanzania |
| **Region** | East Africa |
| **Language** | Maa (ɔl Maa) |
| **Type** | Teaching tale / Moral fable |
| **Moral** | "Emoniata oo nkituak" — Patience is the mother of all virtues |
| **Duration** | 4 minutes |
| **Status** | ✅ Researched, scripted, packaged |

**Summary:** An ancient crocodile tests two twin brothers by asking them to carry water without spilling. The impatient brother runs and loses everything; the patient brother accepts small losses and wins the crocodile's secret wisdom.

**Sources:** Traditional Maasai teaching tale. Lake Natron in Tanzania is referenced as the setting. The crocodile as a wise figure appears in many East African folktales.

**Notes:** The story is a teaching tale about the Maasai value of endurance (enyanyit). The lesson of patience through water-carrying is a metaphor for life's journey.
